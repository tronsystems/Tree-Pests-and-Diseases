Chalara fraxinea
(Ash Dieback)

Chalara dieback of ash is a serious disease of ash trees caused by a fungus called Chalara fraxinea. The disease causes leaf loss and crown dieback in affected trees, and it can kill trees. You'll find information below on how to spot signs of the disease.

At the base of dead side shoots, lesions (or wounds) can often be found on the branches or stem below.

Developing lesions associated with leaf scars.

Developing lesion centred on a dead side shoot.

The wood and pith underlying bark lesions is usually strongly stained.

Lesions which girdle or circle the branch or stem can cause wilting of the foliage above.

Mature trees initially display dieback of the shoots and twigs around their crowns. Dense clumps of foliage may be seen further back on branches where recovery shoots are produced.

Diseased saplings typically have dead tops and/or side shoots.

Dead or dying stem (arrowed) and drying up of leaflets.

Dead or dying leaflets extending into leaflet vein (b) and stem (c).

Between July to October, tiny fruiting bodies of Hymenoscyphus can be found on blackened rachises (leaf stalks) of ash in damp leaves beneath trees. These do not necessarily belong to the Chalara but can be tested to determine their identity.
